Ngā Atua Māori

Ranginui

The sky father, embodying the sky itself. He and Papatūānuku were the primeval parents whose separation by their children allowed light into the world.

Papatūānuku

The earth mother, representing the land. Her union with Ranginui produced the other significant deities, and she is central to the concept of creation and fertility.

Tāne-mahuta

The god of the forest and birds, known for separating his parents to bring light into the world and for creating the first woman from clay.

Tangaroa

The god of the sea, rivers, and marine life, often depicted in a natural opposition to Tāne-mahuta, representing the sea's vast domain.

Tāwhirimātea

The god of weather, controlling winds, storms, and lightning. He expressed his fury over his parents' separation by waging war against his siblings.

Tūmatauenga

The god of war, humanity, and cultivated food. Tūmatauenga is known for his resilience and resourcefulness, advising his siblings on strategies for survival and conflict, often emphasizing the human aspects of courage and survival.
